About Me





Welcome!

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at the Amherst Center for Fundamental Interactions (or ACFI) of the University of Massachusetts in Amherst, MA. As a theoretical physicist, my research focuses on high energy particle physics with an emphasis on its relation to early universe cosmology. Much of this involves searching for the subtle signatures of new particles and fundamental forces possibly hiding in the messy collisional debris produced in experiments such as the Large Hadron Collider and then asking what effect such particles may have had on certain early stages of the evolution of our universe. Before coming to the ACFI, I was a PhD student in the theory group at TRIUMF, Canada's national laboratory for particle and nuclear physics, and the University of British Columbia.



Through my professional research, I have become deeply interested in machine learning and its applications in data science. The skill set for both theoretical particle physics research and data science is virtually the same, i.e., physical intuition + model building + statistical analysis + computing know-how + communication. I see a career in data science as a natural progression of my general interest in solving problems, uncovering hidden patterns, and revealing insights from ever-growing, high volume, multi-variate data. As a physicist, I currently have the ability to study the world in unprecedented detail but, as a data scientist, I would have the ability to affect it. For this reason, I am very excited about any future professional opportunities as a data scientist.

The Big Question:

Our knowledge of the fundamental laws of physics strongly suggests that the big bang should have created matter and antimatter in equal amounts. But instead, all of our observations suggest that virtually everything in the Universe is entirely made of matter. Discovering how this came to be is the overall goal of my research.

Cosmic Frontier:
Electroweak Phase Transition

One of the necessary preconditions for this matter-antimatter asymmetry is a period in the early Universe where thermal equilibrium is lost. The electroweak phase transition, the period in which elementary particles first gain mass by interacting with the Higgs boson, can induce such a loss of equilibrium but requires new undiscovered particles to do so. Part of my research is to identify the conditions under which new particles can, through interacting with the Higgs boson, prompt such a loss of equilibrium during the electroweak phase transition.

Energy Frontier:
Collider Physics

At the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) protons are collided at never-before-seen energies, simulating the conditions of the early Universe. With the recent discovery of the Higgs boson, this opens the possibility of experimentally probing the electroweak phase transition. My research in this area is focused on examining the data for subtle hints possibly left behind by new undiscovered particles associated with equilibrium loss during the electroweak phase transition and, if not possible at the LHC, identifying the requirements for future experiments to discover these particles.

Precision Frontier:
Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay

Lepton number violation can produce the matter-antimatter asymmetry via the Leptogenesis mechanism but also requires the existence of new undiscovered particles. Lepton number violation can be directly probed by both low energy experiments searching for rare neutrinoless double beta decays and high energy experiments such as the LHC. Another part of my research is to determine and compare the statistical sensitivity of each of these experimental approaches to various different new physics models.
